Inner-Outer Loop Theory of Typing

(Crump & Logan, 2010; Logan & Crump, 2009)



This theory divides the cognitive process of typing into two separate hierarchical processes or ‘loops’—the outer loop and the inner loop. The outer loop transforms text or thoughts into a series of words and passes these words to the inner loop. The inner loop is an automatic and subconscious process that transforms each word into a series of coordinated hand and finger movements to make up the necessary keystrokes. The inner loop causes parallel activation of constituent keystrokes for each letter in the word and provides a serial control process (John, 1996; Wu & Liu, 2008). According to this theory, the outer loop is unaware of the processes of the inner-loop. Because of this, typists typically have to think only of the word or sentence rather than thinking of the individual characters or the layout of the keyboard. Hence, typists have little explicit knowledge of what their fingers are doing (Logan & Crump, 2009).

Response Activation Model (RAM)

(Welsh & Elliott, 2004)



All stimuli with actionable potential (e.g., links, images, ads) capture a user’s attention and prime movement responses—or subconsciously program an action by transmitting nerve impulses to the hand and arm muscles—toward or away from the stimulus. As top-down processing ultimately selects the target stimulus and overcomes the subconscious attraction toward nontarget stimuli—referred to as selective inhibition—the interference between the two processing systems results in “longer reaction and movement times in situations when distractor stimuli are present” (Welsh & Elliott, 2004, p. 1032).

Biased Competition Theory (BCT)

(Desimone & Duncan, 1995)



“Attention is an emergent property of many neural mechanisms working to resolve competition for visual processing and control of behavior” (Desimone & Duncan, 1995, p. 194). According to BCT, humans process visual stimuli in two ways: most are processed efficiently and often heuristically or subconsciously by one system, while a second system selectively applies attention to stimuli deemed relevant for accomplishing a given task (Norman & Shallice, 1986; Posner & DiGirolamo, 2000).


Competition for attention resources is central to BCT. In the presence of competing stimuli, the stimulus-driven system draws on these attention resources, thereby reducing working memory capacity (Lavie et al., 2004)--i.e., causes attentional interference (e.g., Cho & Cho, 2021).


Attentional interference is characterized by slower reaction times, longer task completion times, and more mistakes (Fan et al., 2002; Lorenc et al., 2021).

Stochastic Optimizer Submovements Model

(Meyer et al., 1988)




An aimed movement toward a specified target region involves a primary submovement and an optional secondary corrective submovement. The submovements are assumed to be programmed such that they minimize average total movement time while maintaining a high frequency of target hits. The programming process achieves this minimization by optimally adjusting the average magnitudes and durations of noisy neuromotor force pulses used to generate the submovements.
